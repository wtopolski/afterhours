Custom H-bridge

A discrete-transistor H-bridge for driving a small DC motor in both directions. Four switches arranged in an H around the load — the classic topology behind every motor controller, built from parts on the bench.

worked 2026-07 · in progress · v0.1
Overview

What this is

An H-bridge switches the polarity of the voltage applied to a load, letting a DC motor spin forward or reverse from a single-polarity supply. This build is a from-scratch version using discrete transistors rather than an integrated driver — the goal is to see every branch of the circuit, not to hide it behind a chip.

How it works

Four switches, four states

Four switches sit at the corners of an H, with the motor bridging the crossbar. Closing opposite diagonals connects the motor to the supply in one polarity; closing the other diagonal reverses it. The other two useful combinations disconnect the motor (coast) or short its terminals (brake).

Forward

S1 and S4 closed. Current flows through the motor from left to right, spinning it in the forward direction.

S1 + S4 → +V

Reverse

S2 and S3 closed. Current flows through the motor in the opposite direction; polarity is inverted, motor spins the other way.

S2 + S3 → −V

Coast

All switches open. Motor terminals disconnected from the supply; the rotor free-wheels on its own inertia.

open → freewheel

Brake

Both low-side (or both high-side) switches closed. Motor terminals shorted; back-EMF dissipates and the rotor stops quickly.

S3 + S4 → short
Shoot-through — never do this:
S1 + S2 closed simultaneously → supply short-circuit
S3 + S4 closed on the same leg → supply short-circuit

Control logic must interlock same-leg switches. In practice, add dead-time between transitions.
Build

From breadboard to bench

Prototyped first on a breadboard with discrete transistors and resistors, driven by an Arduino UNO R4 running a simple switching loop. Once the four states behaved, the circuit was moved onto perfboard and hand-soldered. Final test bench uses a joystick shield feeding direction and speed into the bridge, spinning a small motor with a 3D-printed propeller.
